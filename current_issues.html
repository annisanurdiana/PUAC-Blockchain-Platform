Current Issues

By PUACMarch 24, 2022

Agricultural supply chains throughout the world have grown into a multi-layered vertically integrated system, with each tier removing more value than the one before it. Despite the fact that supermarkets and commodities corporations continue to declare their support for farmers and customers alike, their dominance over supply chains and distribution limits farmers ability to extract value from the food they produce. Key industry concerns must be addressed in order to overcome these obstacles.

Lack of Transparency and Trust

Global supply chains are intricate networks with significant entry barriers for smaller market players. This complication also implies that the more agricultural products progress through the supply chain, the more difficult it becomes to track their progress to market. Dealing directly with global consumers is now unworkable for most local farmers. Local farmers are forced to rely on multinational middlemen to export their crops due to currency exchange risk, contract re-negotiations, buyer payment defaults, difficult documentation, and costly administrative overheads. Increased demand for single-origin goods provides farmers with additional opportunity to maximize the value of their products' quality. Many agricultural producers are beginning to capitalize on the rising demand for single origin products. 

Consumers, on the other hand, are still given minimal documentation to establish the provenance of their products. With rising global consumer wealth, traceability and complete transparency of the manufacturing and supply chain are becoming more important in customer purchase decisions. More consumers around the world are requesting information because they have a right to know where their food comes from, how it was delivered, and what manufacturing procedures were employed. Chemicals, insecticides, and gases used in the manufacture and delivery of food items across the whole supply chain are currently unknown to consumers.

Finance Obstacles

Barriers to agricultural finance increase farmers' capital expenses. The farmer's cost of capital is influenced by high fees, long approval processes, needless paperwork, inadequate banking policies, and regulatory expenditures. Potential funders are unable to receive an exact, unmodified record of a farmer's stock on hand, including both the quality and current worth, because there is no mechanism in place to adequately record data. 

As a result, some farmers are obliged to take out high-interest overdrafts and bridging loans to keep their enterprises a float. Insurance firms are wary of insuring agricultural goods after it has been harvested. It is impossible for insurers to grasp the farmer's definite status, particularly the number and quality of stock on hand, without trustworthy and accessible data. Due to insufficient data, the insurer is vulnerable to unethical practices, ownership risk, data manipulation, and a general lack of transparency.

Insufficient Data Handling

On a single, independent, worldwide platform, no industry-wide software solution exists to serve farmers and essential auxiliary firms. The lack of a centralized platform for important agricultural supply chain partners to produce, exchange, and keep data. To handle their production, stockpiles, marketing, and general operations, an alarming proportion of farmers and logistics providers continue to rely on rudimentary spreadsheets, paper records, and dockets.
